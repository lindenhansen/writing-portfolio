In the water so cold it eats my bones and being all in one fell swoop. Mountain runoff. It seeps into my pores and the frigid feeling beats my lungs to breath. On the banks of the crystalline lake my friends all giggle and swarm to and fro in bare feet and wet-hanging hair. A lulling frequency of high pitched chittering bounces along the water, skitters up the crags, and echoes out along the jagged skyline.

I find a familiar plunge into the icy abyss. It all stops and the world turns to muffled syllables and mere descriptions in big bubble letters. Here I have nothing but the blue and the tingling in my throat when the time has run out.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

I invite a motley crew to stake out by the ponds and drink stolen beers. Snake River Brewing, because that's the fancy one my aunt and uncle get when they're in town. And no one else drinks in my family so they stay preserved by chilled ice in the fridge in my grandmother's basement.

They're received by youthful hands quivering with anticipation. To crack a cold one. To become one of those with swaggering stature and a knowing squinted eye. But soon hands quiver with cold.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

Hailey and I swipe two beers but I don't remember which kind, and hide in her grandma's guest house. Taking sips and feeling royal. Feeling like the whole world just licked me right up and as the buttery taste burns my throat, I think yeah… this is what girls are for.

Swinging our feet over the loft ledge. Feeling warm and fuzzy like a no-longer-virgin.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

We administer stick and poke tattoos in the back seat of a Gator four wheeler, encompassed by tall reeds on all sides. Aspen leaves rustle through the breeze. Oh, just pray this one stays. If it fades, I swear I'll do it over again. I'll keep this time forever. I'll swallow it whole.

Beatrix, Jocelyn, and I, we all do miniature portraits of boobs right at the top rungs of our ribs. All cartoon-y like the ones on makeup bags bought off Amazon. My neck burns from curling down to peer at the needle as it plunges each time. Bea's turns out utterly lopsided— one outsizing the other by probably two whole sizes. We laugh because it's a metaphor. Visibility for women in parallel plights.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

We are little rascals spinning around in the tall dry grass, tripping over in our dirt-dusted cowboy boots— two sizes too small but worn in good enough, dripped out in red embroidery. Stitches spiral this way and that as far as the sky will take us. And sequined too if we feel like it. Some don teals, blues, greens, and golds. All the colors of God's own natural rainbow.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

Sometimes we camp next to the small creek that runs through the horse pasture— just a short tromp from the back porch and then we're there, deep in a wooded grove. On all fours, the largest stones must be removed to make way for the bones of spines. We sleep in the dirt with queen duvets wrested from our own beds. It is dark now and the stars light up this valley. I see the Milky Way.

Flames from the campfire we've scavenged— and the campfire our parents told us we're not to have— flick at the heavens. Ellie brought cold spaghetti and meatballs in a tupperware. It tastes much better thanks to the weed she brought too.

We talk about the Blair Witch and whether or not she'll get us out here.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

We go out there to the ranch one night in my friend's borrowed car, but end up doing doughnuts in the Stilson lot. Popped tire. Security car. Mad dash. And then school lockers the next morning. Eyes blazing from whiskey. Angry parents. They make me break up with my boyfriend Sebastian because I need to spend more time with Jesus. They force me to do it. To give names. And then they force me to come clean and tell my friends I was the one who gave the names. A big fat hole punctured in my heart then, I think.

I can't quite scrape the bottom of this one.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

My parents say no to the rest of the eighth grade because of the trouble I'm in but it's ok because everyone else follows suit anyway. Everyone goes online. I waste away bitter winter months waiting for summer again, when I can go to the river and burn my skin in the hot sun and think about what terrible thing I'll do next.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

Hailey is leaving to go back to Florida soon and I need to see her once more because I'll burst if I don't so a meet up is secretly arranged. But my mom gets wind and intercepts me. Fuming and unsure what to do with this child.

If I'm not a friend, I don't know who I am. It's my safety net. We're best friends and we do everything together and that's just the way it is and if I'm told anything different, if any bit of it splinters or deviates toward another truth, I might as well be lost.

And I cannot be lost.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

The amount of times I walk those irrigation ditches alone at sunset. When my family first starts to leave during the hot, dry months and I stay to work. When my heart breaks clean and whole and just into a million pieces. The sun glows red reflected off water and I'm in a bright blue zip-up hoodie Jocelyn left in my back seat.

I would go like this.

Come home from work smelling like pastries and coffee beans. Take off my apron. Grab Marlboro Golds and a lighter adorned with a kitten and cherry blossoms. Start walking. Listen.

Birds, leaves, the sky, the earth, the dirt crumbling, the grass blades talking to each other, the core of me bleeding out.

Out of the woods now. Hit dirt road now. Open green rickety gate. In a field so big I feel a lump in my throat each time my eyes meet it. It hugs me. I light up. Big puffs because my soul is, in fact, still a wasteland. And just walk straight. Straight down that stony path under that big blue sky in that hole of a place where all I ever know is it's getting a little harder to breathe. From smoke or from solitude, I don't know.

Find a puddle in the grass where it didn't drain quite just yet and then I sit on the wood-post fence and stare at the mountain range and cry.

I don't pick up my phone.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

It is not a home— where I've been. Simply a space to park and sit with palms upturned for a while, holding my love. Like a street beggar but the opposite.

Please come partake.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

Wet grass on my bare thighs now because I've gone skinny dipping again in the creek. Maddie and Emily squeeze up next to me and tell me how cool we are. How we're so cool and we must go thrifting together and look for real good finds.

Because we are real good friends.

We can sashay across the wood paneled floors in our flowing skirts with pleats up the sides. Ribbons tied to our wrists. Dance in the dressing room to a song that sounds like a glistening azure coast and flower-adorned berets atop our shiny heads.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

After Music on Main we all trickle back to the cabin in a caravan of cars over the pass from Idaho to Wyoming. We stay up late into the night, Beatrix DJing and oh so many unexpected arrivals. A true party. A good one.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

But there is also a baby Elk in a field somewhere. And it will stay like a memory stain. Warm felted ears in my hands. A detail I cannot quite place but that requires placing still. I mull it over again and again, mushing it up in my mouth and spitting out back out, seeing which form it takes this time. An order from God. A command from the forest. A coyote's own request for deliverance. Or the wind asking me to lift my sticky fingers from its path. A hoard of Aspen clones saying make way for what will be.

I am 10 years old and the sun is hot. Stinging and buzzing. Sparkling across the Wyoming cattle pasture. No stock in sight, though. Only three wispy figures. Two elk— mother and baby. A coyote prancing giddily in the distance. I shield my eyes from the piercing rays and watch the scene unfold.
Coyote creeps closer, eyes beady with hunger… momma Elk freaks. She thunders away, screeching, beckoning her spawn to follow. It doesn't. Instead, it collapses under the hopeless shade of a rickety wood-post fence. Minutes pass, Carter wields a shotgun. POW. One warning and the coyote skips out. A mouthful of lean elk meat is not worth the spray of metal. After the scatter, only the baby's shape remains.
The orb ablaze in the sky inches closer to afternoon. Nail-biting ensues. Hours in the sun welcome death for freshly-born beings. We watch the calf's ribs rise and fall at a leaden pace, eclipsed by the tall grass. Mom is nowhere in sight. Was she spooked by the sound of firepower? Is she mistakenly mourning the loss of her spawn to predator jowls?
We must do something. We are saviors. Protectors of the wild…
And then we are in the truck bed– quivering hooves sounding against its floor. We take the thing to a stall in the barn nearby. Feed it milk from a makeshift bottle. It trickles down my arm, steaming warm. Sustenance breathes a spark into the animal's black eyes. They bleed with abandonment, loneliness, and fear. But I see peril by another name, and the truth unravels. Cherished ideas of nature's perfect balance crumble quietly. 10 years of butterflies and bees burnt to ash by one second in those eyes. My hand is the inflictor, not nature's hot sun. Not the elements, it is us.
We stumble back home, hearts weary and confused. A hole ripped in the fragile tapestry. We dared embark further than man's designated stature…
Then a sight in the window makes our hearts drop to our knees. Bursting through delusion's cloudy surface, reality strikes hard in the face. Mom is back, searching for her child, echoing painful coos. Desperately, intuitively, demandingly. She is here to perform her own rescue mission.
Keys in the ignition. Back to the barn. Scoop the calf into the truck. Deliver it to the field. Pray to the skies, misty-eyed.
One second passes— Is the reek of human stench too strong? Is there forgiveness here in this tall grass? We wait. And then it happens, they trot together, sounding mews of reunion, into the hazy yellow evening like a sigh of relief.

All my friends have gone now and it's just me, my boots and the dirt road. But at the end of the day that's what it's always been. I spend the last month before California topless under the sun re-reading The Road and other books about hungry addicts. Listening to Patty Griffin.

*And if the pain ends when you die*
*And I wonder if there was*
*Some better way to say goodbye*

They still sit pretty under my left breast. A little faded and in need of a touchup. Very faded indeed.

The dormitory is small but relatively spacious. Two twin beds. A carpet from the 50s covers the floor in its entirety. I'd rather hardwood. But the storage is spacious. A breeze comes through the South-facing window. A mini fridge whirs once plugged into the socket in the wall. It's quiet.

Months later the bookshelves are populated by sprawling titles and the window stays open each night. Passersby shout and sing to their favorite songs and the girls within the room laugh at their fizzled attempts at melancholy voice. A slight mention of a half-baked idea turns into torrents of thought and an eventual 4AM bedtime. Conversations are fueled by trail mix. The occasional milk chocolate bar.

Little trinkets grace the realm.

Three succulents. A giftcard. A sticky note reading "houseparty". A sailboat on a takeout box. A half-burned candle. A lighter with a kitten and cherry blossoms on it.

<div class="section-break">❖</div>

What girls are for is putting the pedal to the floor and saying prayers to God with the windows down as the 2005 Toyota Landcruiser whips over every unseen mystery. Give it a pothole or a boulder or a chisler. Then scurry in its wake. In my periphery, golden strands of hair beat reflected sun rays in a race to the sky. Gently they whisper secrets to the blue. But so loud they scream truths in my ears. Race to the end. Bludgeon blindly against the sagebrush. A bumper and a dream.

Or they are for a cup of chamomile tea at a chestnut desk in the middle of the night. A lamp in the corner casting yellow shadows longer than the hour but not quite long enough to shield the little opening in my eye. You can see my heart beating soft in my chest if you peer down into it. My knees lose blood because they're bent so attentively— nuzzled up to my chin as I listen to her hopes and dreams and fears and all.

It's a true space of comfort. I let the warmth seep into me now.
